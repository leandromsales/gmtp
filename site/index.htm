Global Media Transmission Protocol (GMTP)

Leandro Melo de Sales

 

      The Global Media Transmission Protocol (GMTP) is a cross-layer network protocol operating in the transport and network layers of the TCP/IP stack for supporting large-scale transmission of live streaming content over the Internet. GMTP constitutes a network of cooperative routers and they disseminate the media packets according to the demand of their clients. The cooperation between routers are determined by the streaming servers, based on the bandwidth of network channels explicitly shared by the routers already in use to disseminate content, obtained and updated periodically. In the context of GMTP, a router is called relay and the protocol is organized as depicted in Figure 1.

1.   Overview and Motivation

      Real time multimedia delivery on the Internet is essential for the many current applications such as WebTV. A hybrid Peer-to-Peer (P2P) / Content Delivery Networks (CDN) architecture is the most efficient approach in robust system for large-scale multimedia distribution over the Internet. In Live Streaming Systems (LSS) based on P2P/CDN architecture, a data-source processes the captured media and transmits data packets to CDN servers often deployed in multiple locations, over multiple backbones and ISPs. Upon connecting to one of these servers to start playing the media, Internet clients not only receive the media content, but also become a media relay and share the content with other clients. As depicted in Figure 2, this constitutes a P2P network, where clients connect directly to each other and also play the same media, indirectly from one of the CDN servers. From the network perspective, the solutions concentrate efforts on improving the protocols throughput for delivering data to multiple nodes at a time. In this context, it is more desirable to keep the data flow and reproduce the content as it arrives than waiting for retransmission of lost packets.

 

Figure 2: Network arquitecture used in P2P/CDN live streaming systems.

 

      The current processes involved to distribute live content over the Internet promote an unfair competition between heterogeneous devices. In essence, they prioritize fixed-IP machines in a highly complex and dynamic network traffic generated by these devices, where the systems uses the best effort IP service to disseminate content, causing duplicated transmissions of the same flow. As a consequence, it increases network resource consumption and degrades the quality of service provided to the applications.

      This problem becomes increasingly critical when considering resource-limited devices, where the problem aforementioned provides serious concerns for end-users of devices such as smart phones, TVs and tablets. Recent estimations show that worldwide mobile traffic will increase 13-fold between 2012 and 2017, a rate of 66% per year or almost three times of the fixed-IP machines. These systems avoid mobile devices because of their nature of mobility and resource constraints, but due to their popularization, a solution is indispensable to enable different types of devices to fairly use network resources, from computers to any type of consumer electronic devices.

      In this context, an important challenge is how to define P2P/CDN networks to offer resources regardless of the device type and the application layer protocols, as well as encourage end-users to share network resources (e.g. upload bandwidth) with others, while they watch a live event. In this context, consider four important aspects:

 

1.     The opportunistic approach of live streaming systems is not feasible: end-users will not share their resources with others when they are with idle upload bandwidth and are not interested in watching a live streaming of interest to others. This is a typical scenario that P2P/CDN systems rely on: users need to be interested in receiving a stream to allow the system to ask them to share the content with others. A potential relay is useless when it has no interest in receiving a stream, resulting in a waste of idle resources (no interest, no sharing). This problem becomes increasingly critical when there are end-users connected to the system acting as free-riders, consuming resources from the system without sharing their resources with other end-users. A successful solution should compensate users that share resources in some way regardless of their interest in the content, while encouraging free-riders to contribute to the distribution of the live content;

2.     Limitation of computing resources: in general, a Resource Limited (RL) device such as smart phones and smart TVs is not able to share as much of its computational resources with other devices when compared to Resource Abundant (RA) devices line desktops and laptops. The problem becomes increasingly critical considering the mobility facilities of RL devices, which increases the churn in the P2P network and hence impacting on the service. Since RA devices are better fit for sharing more resources with other nodes, the system can reduce media glitches and playback startup time. Also, when RL nodes dominate the system, the P2P overlay network can get clogged, which hampers its growth and makes the system become highly dependent on CDN servers. A successful solution should normalize the available resources based on the network perspective, instead of considering the available resources on each device;

3.     Network protocols used in LSS are not suitable: the LSS are based on network protocols (TCP, UDP, DCCP [6]) that were not developed for such kind of service. In this manner, the application layer should implement almost all functions provided by LSS, which causes incompatibilities among them and increases the number of solutions for the same purpose. A successful solution should rely on good practices that have been instituted over the years on the Internet, such as those considered for the Web. The Web does not depend on the applications (browser) to transport the data from the client to the server. Instead, it uses TCP to separate the data transmission from the way the data will be exposed to end-users, which is an application decision regardless of the type of the end-users' devices;

4.     Information-Centric Networking (ICN) is a great candidate for provisioning device independent network services: ICN has received significant attention in recent years, mainly driven by the fact that distributing and manipulating information has become the major function on the Internet today. Unlike the traditional host-centric networking paradigm, where information is obtained by contacting specified named hosts, ICN aims at providing native network primitives for efficient information retrieval. It directly names and operates on information objects. The goal is to provide a network infrastructure service better suited to todays use (in particular, content distribution and mobility) and more resilient to disruptions and failures [5], [7]. 

 

      Based on the aforementioned aspects, the Global Media Transmission Protocol (GMTP) was designed to compensate the necessary features for live media distribution over a datagram-oriented network, such as Internet. It decouples the way to transport application data from the way they are delivered to and rendered by end-users applications, similar to the Web, as discussed previously. The way that devices get connected to receive a live streaming is also transparent from how the data is distributed on a collaborative P2P/CDN network.

 

      GMTP is a result of an extensive investigation of the network protocols available in todays Internet, targeting on improving communication issues present in previous protocols for transmitting live data, such as TCP, UDP, DCCP, SCTP, and so forth. It supports unicast and multicast transmission of congestion-controlled datagrams over an overlay network built in the network layer of the TCP/IP stack, allowing the use of a new concept called P2P sockets in the transport layer. For this case, when a client node expects to receive a stream, it sends a requesting packet specifying the data source node that is transmitting the event. Based on this information, the GMTP transparently gets stream data from peers connected to the overlay network by matching those already receiving the same data flow, according to their capacity of contributions in terms of bandwidth of the known routes from clients to servers, where new partnerships are defined based on route intersections. In this way, GMTP allows the communication of peers from different vendors and applications, regardless of the application architecture.

 

      In this context, GMTP efficiently uses the available network resources by clustering nodes with interests in common so that they can cooperate among them targeting a single goal: watching a live streaming event. For this, GMTP provides a novel host-to-host and host-to-server connection process; it supports dual mode transmission chosen automatically according to the nodes demand for a given live stream; and it makes use of a network assisted congestion control algorithm called Rate Control Protocol (RCP), with a more conservative use of network resources while downloading and uploading data packets, regardless of the type and computational resources available on the end-user devices.

3.   Main features of GMTP

1.     Access to a broadcast of a live media through a process of three-way hand-share (3WHS), where the existing relays in the route between the client and the server is authorized to intercept the request packet in case the streaming is already being processed and routed to another relay.

2.     Constitution of a network of cooperative relays transparent to the application. A relay executed a process called Registration of Participation, which allows relays to participate of the cooperative network by notifying the stream servers their intentions to participate of the network and/or for receiving a specific live streaming.

3.     Allow server and client applications to send and receive streams shared between nodes in the same network through the use of multicast, while it uses unicast to transport stream data between distinct administrative domains (networks), avoiding the ratio of one connection per client to the server.

4.     Allow different applications share the same media streaming. If different applications A (eg. standalone) and B (eg. embedded web object) are interested in receiving the same media stream, GMTP automatically shares the content among them.

5.     Congestion control for unicast data flows, based on the lower bandwidth of the existing relays between the client and server, named Unicast Congestion Control (GMTP-UCC), and Congestion Control for multicast data flows, based on reports submitted by client nodes, named Multicast Congestion Control (GMTP-MCC).

6.     Discovery of relay partners between different networks and support for negotiating partnership; supported the formation of partnerships based on network metrics , such as the end-to-end transmission capacity.

7.     Verification of data integrity through the use of digital signatures provided by the servers, thus preventing pollution attacks.

8.     Support for auxiliary functions, such as election of reporter nodes and tolerance for node disconnections.

 

4.   General Architecture

      GMTP is composed by two modules called GMTP-Intra and GMTP-Inter, which operate at the transport layer and network, respectively, thus defining its architecture, shown in 4.

 

Figure 4: General architecture of GMTP protocol.

      The responsibilities of GMTP-Intra and GMTP-Inter are the following:

GMTP-Intra: it provides services to network applications in order to abstract the complexity of common tasks to any end-system, such as multi-point connecting, datagram multiplexing/demultiplexing between application/transport layers and between transport/network layers, as well as control congestion of multicast data flows. This module comprises GMTP instance running on the client operating system, accessible via a socket API, such as POSIX/BSD. In this context, a socket is the representation of an instance of the GMTP, which is responsible for managing all communication activities relevant to the external environment (other GMTP applications). In the context of a connection, the GMTP-Intra maintains several variables related to state implementation of algorithms for connection management (establishment and disconnection), multicast congestion control, multiplexing/demultiplexing, election of reporter nodes and determining the form and functions for allowing application to generate and read the media description, by allowing the application to set the values ​​of the parameters or obtain access to their values.

 

GMTP-Inter: it provide the P2P network of favor composed of routers, which act as bridges for accessing CDN network servers. It is executed in the routers, which cooperatively constitute the overlay network according to the common interest for a given live media. The stream servers communicate directly with the routers, asking them to form partnership to disseminate the content to the client nodes. In the context of a connection, the GMTP-Inter maintains state variables related to the functions of its responsibility, such as establishing a connection between routers or servers; selection and management of cooperative routers; election of report nodes; sharing of multimedia streams; and congestion control assisted by the network. Furthermore, it allows the definition of initial configuration parameters for the network of favors and integration with one or more CDNs servers, as depicted in Figure 5. In this case, the router administrator can define a set of configuration parameters such as registration to participation in one or more CDNs networks; the maximum bandwidth (download and upload) that the router is authorized to use and share; the period that the router is authorized to join the network of favors, in days and hours; the maximum number of partnerships that can be performed and maximum number of data streams that can be shared; parameters of the equations used to execute the congestion control algorithms; maximum size of the media cache to be shared; and settings about the digital certificates provided by the server, such as automatic download and store of digital certificates.
